Telescopes in India

India hosts several world-class observatories and utilizes various types of telescopes including Optical, Radio, and Space-based observatories to study the universe.

Types of Telescopes

Optical Telescopes: Gather visible light. Used for observing stars and galaxies.

Radio Telescopes: Detect radio waves from space. Useful for studying pulsars and black holes.

Space Telescopes: Located beyond Earth's atmosphere to avoid distortion and detect radiation blocked by the atmosphere.

Major Indian Telescopes

AstroSat

Type: Space Multi-wavelength Observatory

Operator: ISRO

Launched: September 28, 2015

India's first dedicated multi-wavelength space observatory, launched by ISRO in 2015. It enables simultaneous observation of the universe in ultraviolet, optical, and X-ray bands. AstroSat has been instrumental in studying black holes, neutron stars, and star formation in distant galaxies, providing data that helps astronomers understand high-energy processes in the cosmos.

Giant Metrewave Radio Telescope (GMRT)

Type: Radio Telescope Array

Location: Pune, Maharashtra

Established: 1995

The Giant Metrewave Radio Telescope (GMRT), located near Pune, is one of the world's most sensitive radio telescope arrays at low frequencies. Consisting of 30 fully steerable parabolic dishes, each 45 meters in diameter, it investigates a wide range of astrophysical problems, including pulsars, supernovae, and the formation of galaxies in the early universe.

Himalayan Chandra Telescope (HCT)

Type: Optical-Infrared

Location: Hanle, Ladakh

First Light: 2000

The Himalayan Chandra Telescope (HCT) is located at the Indian Astronomical Observatory in Hanle, Ladakh, at a staggering altitude of 4,500 meters. Operated by the Indian Institute of Astrophysics, this 2-meter optical-infrared telescope takes advantage of the pristine, dry, and dark skies of the Himalayas to observe faint stars, galaxies, and nebulae.

Aditya-L1

Type: Solar Observatory (Space)

Operator: ISRO

Launched: September 2, 2023

Aditya-L1 is India's pioneering solar mission, designed to observe the Sun from a halo orbit around the first Lagrange point (L1), 1.5 million km from Earth. It carries seven payloads to study the photosphere, chromosphere, and the outermost layer of the Sun (the corona), helping scientists understand solar flares, coronal mass ejections, and space weather dynamics.

International Telescopes

Hubble Space Telescope

Type: Space Telescope (Optical/UV/NIR)

Operator: NASA / ESA

Launched: April 24, 1990

Launched in 1990, the Hubble Space Telescope has revolutionized our understanding of the universe. Orbiting Earth outside the distortion of the atmosphere, it captures incredibly sharp images of deep space objects. Its discoveries include determining the rate of expansion of the universe and discovering that nearly every major galaxy is anchored by a black hole.

James Webb Space Telescope (JWST)

Type: Space Telescope (Infrared)

Operator: NASA / ESA / CSA

Launched: December 25, 2021

The James Webb Space Telescope is the largest and most powerful space telescope ever built. Launched in 2021, it observes the universe in infrared light, allowing it to peer through cosmic dust and see the first galaxies formed after the Big Bang. It also analyzes the atmospheres of exoplanets to search for building blocks of life.

Very Large Telescope (VLT)

Type: Optical Telescope Array

Location: Atacama Desert, Chile

Operator: ESO

Operated by the European Southern Observatory (ESO) in the Atacama Desert of Chile, the VLT consists of four individual telescopes, each with a primary mirror 8.2 meters across. These telescopes can work together to form a giant interferometer, allowing astronomers to see details up to 25 times finer than with individual telescopes.

W. M. Keck Observatory

Type: Optical/Infrared

Location: Mauna Kea, Hawaii

Established: 1993

Located on the summit of Mauna Kea in Hawaii, the W. M. Keck Observatory features two 10-meter telescopes, the largest optical/infrared telescopes in the world. They use adaptive optics to correct for atmospheric turbulence, producing images of comparable sharpness to those from space telescopes.
